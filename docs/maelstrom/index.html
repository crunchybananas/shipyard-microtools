MAELSTROM

THE WHIRLPOOL THAT SWALLOWS LIGHT

What you are looking at

A black hole, computed honestly. For every pixel, this page fires a ray of light backwards from your eye and integrates it step by step through the spacetime of a rotating (Kerr) black hole until it escapes to the stars, lands on the glowing disk of in-falling matter, or crosses the event horizon and is never seen again.

The strange things you see are not effects. They are consequences.

The machinery

Light follows null geodesics — the straightest possible paths through curved spacetime. Each ray is evolved with a 4th-order Runge–Kutta integrator under the Kerr Hamiltonian (Boyer–Lindquist coordinates, G = c = M = 1):

2H = Δ⁄Σ pr² + pθ²⁄Σ + gφφL² − 2gtφEL + gttE² = 0

with energy E and angular momentum L conserved along the ray, Σ = r²+a²cos²θ and Δ = r²−2r+a². Set the spin a to zero and this collapses exactly to the Schwarzschild solution.

Why the disk is brighter on one side

The disk orbits at a respectable fraction of the speed of light — at the inner edge, roughly half of it. Matter racing toward you beams its light forward like a headlamp; matter racing away dims and reddens. Both follow from one number, the relativistic g-factor:

g = 1 ⁄ [ut(E − ΩL)]   ·   Iobs = g⁴ Iem   ·   Tobs = g·Tem

Each patch of disk is a blackbody at temperature T(r) ∝ r−3/4(1−√(rin/r))1/4 (the Shakura–Sunyaev profile); its colour is the Planck colour of g·T. Toggle beaming and redshift in the dock and compare. That asymmetric blaze is relativity, doing arithmetic.

The thin bright ring

At r = 1.5 rs (for zero spin) photons can orbit the black hole in circles. Light that grazes this photon sphere can loop once, twice, many times before escaping to your eye — so you see the disk again and again, infinitely thinner each time, stacked against the shadow's edge. You also see the disk behind the hole, bent over the top and under the bottom: that is the halo.

Spin

Drag the spin slider. A rotating black hole drags spacetime around with it — orbits in the dragged direction survive closer in, so the inner edge of the disk (the ISCO) crawls inward from 6M toward the horizon, and the shadow goes lopsided. At a/M = 0.998 you are looking at the fastest black hole nature is believed to build.

The probe

Drop a probe and watch the two clocks. Its own clock τ ticks at its usual pace — falling feels like nothing. But measured by your clock t, the probe slows, its signal stretches and reddens, and at the horizon it freezes mid-fall, fading forever. Both clocks are integrated from the same geodesic equations as the picture. Neither clock is lying. That is rather the point.
