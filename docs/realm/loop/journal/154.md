## the argument

Every audio cue is declared "silent-by-default,
miss-able" and gets a gain peak of 0.04-0.10. Their
duration is ≤6s. They fire at most once per realm
(8 of 9 are once-per-realm). They have **no UI** —
nothing tells the player a sound just played.

In a play session of ~15-30 minutes, a user hears
maybe 2-4 of 9 cues. On laptop speakers that can't
reproduce 72 Hz (great-storm root), on audio contexts
that Chrome throttles when the tab is hidden.

**How many cues does a real user actually
DISCRIMINATE vs just register as "some ambient
sound?"** The catalog has 9 affect labels on paper
(tension / finality / discovery / ceremony / quiet-
wonder / resolution / intergenerational / pastoral /
distant-past) but no evidence they LAND as 9
distinct emotional readings.

112's design philosophy set 5 principles:
1. silent by default
2. miss-able
3. once per realm
4. distinct fingerprints
5. no UI

Principles 1-3 and 5 are adversarial to player
attention. Principle 4 is the one verifiable thing —
"distinct fingerprints" — and 137 filed a review
asking "is principle 1/2 still right as the catalog
grows?" The answer has not been sought.

The acoustic-contrast table in audio-surfaces.md
shows the cues are dimensionally distinct (Hz, wave,
structure, attack, decay, character). That's a
code-level fingerprint. It is NOT a perceptual
fingerprint. A user with headphones might read all
mid-register sines as "some kind of bell" regardless
of whether the code calls them requiem / stone /
founders / namesake / shepherds.

## dead weight candidate

Not a cue. The PROCESS is the dead weight: shipping
new cues before verifying old ones are
distinguishable is writing into a vacuum.

**Concretely:**
- **Before shipping a 10th cue** — constellation,
  night-shape, longest-night are all filed — pause
  and run 149's live-audition review.
- The audition needs real speakers + sequential
  playback + a listener who didn't design the cues
  describing what they heard.
- If 3+ cues land as distinct: audio axis is
  earning its place; continue.
- If users conflate cues: rollback. Keep the
  high-contrast ones (nightmare dissonance,
  requiem bell, first-snow noise — the three
  acoustic outliers); merge the mid-register sines.

## but this could be wrong

Counterarguments I tried to steelman:

1. **Audio-surfaces.md IS the verification.** No —
   design-intent docs prove the author cares, not
   that the design lands.
2. **Headphone users hear more.** Possible, but no
   evidence collected. MANIFEST is a browser game
   primarily played on device speakers.
3. **Each cue is cheap (≤50 LoC).** True. But
   cumulative doc + patterns + acoustic-contrast
   table maintenance is not cheap. Each new cue is
   +25 LoC in audio-surfaces.md. Nine cues in, the
   doc is 466 lines.
4. **The cues are INSURANCE for future audio-
   enabled players.** Then they also need
   verification BEFORE those players arrive.

None of these refute the core: the process has
grown aggressively in one direction without a
single reality-check.
