## method

`G.debug.renderBuildingIsolated(type, { season, daylight: 1.0 })`
× 20 buildings × 4 seasons = 80 canvases. For each:
- peakY / bottomY / width / height
- pixel count (non-bg)
- "snow-cap" pixel count (pale-blue-white threshold)

Previous silhouette/color audits skipped the season
axis. Only thing drawBuilding varies by season is
the winter snow-cap (render.js:2934+). This tick
measures its impact.

## findings

### FINDING 1 — spring = summer = autumn, bit-for-bit

Every non-winter season produced IDENTICAL
silhouette data for every building. 20 buildings
× 3 identical seasons = confirmed. drawBuilding
only branches on `G.season === 'winter'` (for
snow caps). Any non-winter visual variation lives
at render.js top-level (sky tint, tileShift on
terrain) — not inside drawBuilding. The helper
paints a constant grass background, so seasonal
terrain variation doesn't show.

This reframes the seasonal-audit space: **only
spring-vs-winter comparisons are interesting
through the helper**. The other 3 pairs are
trivially identical.

### FINDING 2 — snow-caps add ~112px uniformly

For ~14 of 20 buildings, the winter snow-cap
adds exactly **+112 pixels** (or +113, jitter
noise):

```
house       winter Δ +112
tavern      +118
bakery      +69 (partial — chimney overlaps)
blacksmith  +75 (partial)
windmill    +52 (partial — blade overlap)
lumber      +123
archery     +115
market      +158  (but starting point was 126 from
                   existing white awning pixels)
barracks    +29
tradingpost +109
chickencoop +158 (and +112 snow delta)
cowpen      +156
school      +0   (but +119 snow pixels — cap is
                   drawn on already-present plaster)
castle      +0   (snow below existing spire peak)
church      +77  (+74 snow, smaller cap)
tower       +9   (+61 snow; narrow cap w=8 per 2938)
fisherman   +60  (+113 snow; deck-stilt overlap)
well        +157 (and peakY shifts 77→70)
granary     +158 (peakY shifts 79→70)
farm        +0   (excluded from snow per 2934)
quarry      (excluded too, not in my sample)
```

The uniform +112 is from `drawBuilding`'s snow
template: `fillStyle='rgba(230,240,255,0.85)'` +
`ctx.ellipse(s.x, snowY, 14, 3)` — a 14-wide by
~6-tall ellipse, ~110 px area. Same for most
buildings.

### FINDING 3 — well + granary silhouettes GROW in winter

Two buildings whose total silhouette HEIGHT
increases in winter (not just winter-coloring —
actual new pixels above the previous peak):

```
granary: peakY 79 → 70 (9 pixels taller in winter)
well:    peakY 77 → 70 (7 pixels taller)
```

Both are low-profile buildings. Their native
tops are below y-28 (where the default snow cap
sits). Snow cap extends ABOVE their sprite, making
their winter silhouette structurally different.

**This is a net win for readability at winter.**
At non-winter, well and granary have distinct
shapes but similar peak heights. In winter:
- **well**: peakY=70, gets snow-cap ellipse
  above the rim
- **granary**: peakY=70, also gets snow-cap but
  over its domed top

They converge on peakY=70 in winter. Does that
make them LESS distinguishable? Checking width
and total pixels:
- well winter: 1409 px
- granary winter: 1337 px
- Similar shape profile. New winter-blur pair?

Filed as 086 MEDIUM.

### FINDING 4 — farm and quarry never get snow

render.js:2934 excludes `farm` and `quarry` from
snow-caps explicitly. A winter farm looks
identical to a summer farm (confirmed — all 4
seasons bit-for-bit equal). Quarry presumably
same (not in my test because 046 didn't include
it, but render.js:2934 excludes it).

**For players in a winter realm, farms don't
read as "winter farms."** They read as plain
grass tiles with scarecrows. If realm wants its
seasonal variation to feel consistent, farm snow
might be worth adding. Small drawing change.

### FINDING 5 — tower snow is distinctly smaller

render.js:2937-2938: tower gets `snowW=8`
(vs default 14). Cap adds only ~61 pixels (vs
template ~112). Tower winter silhouette stays
narrower than most buildings' caps would be.

For a pixel-audit bot this is a helpful
differentiator: the tower's winter cap is ~half
the size of a house cap. For a player's eye, less
obvious — but the shape is different.

### FINDING 6 — church snow has cross

render.js:2937: church uses `snowY = s.y - 42`
(taller than default -28) and default width,
but the church cross is drawn ABOVE the cap at
s.y-44 to s.y-46. Snow pixels count: 74 (less
than template 112) because the ellipse at the
tall snowY is partially BEHIND the steeple and
cross.

Structurally: church in winter reads as "church
with cap + cross still visible above." Most
distinctive winter silhouette in the game.

### FINDING 7 — residential 046-cluster still blurs in winter

046 found house ≈ tavern ≈ bakery share silhouette
family. 051 fixed the tavern-vs-house distinction
via the tavern-flag. In winter data:

```
house winter:   peakY 64, px 1540, snow 112
tavern winter:  peakY 55, px 1688, snow 113
bakery winter:  peakY 43, px 1697, snow 112
lumber winter:  peakY 55, px 1629, snow 112
windmill winter: peakY 55, px 1537, snow 112
market winter:  peakY 55, px 1558, snow 238 (awning)
```

All six get +112 snow-cap. Winter doesn't
disambiguate — adding a same-sized cap on top of
residential-family silhouettes just makes them
ALL taller by the same amount.

051's tavern-flag peakY delta (64→55 vs house)
persists in winter (peakY 55 vs 64). Still the
primary differentiator.

Winter's only-partial-differentiation: market
has DOUBLE snow (awning + cap). Market stands out
more in winter than summer.

## summary

1. Only spring↔winter pair shows drawBuilding
   variation; seasons 1-3 are trivially
   identical.
2. Winter adds ~112 px snow-cap to most buildings.
3. Well and granary silhouettes GROW in winter
   (peakY extends up).
4. Farm + quarry get no winter visual cue.
5. Tower and church have distinct snow
   geometries.
6. 046's residential blur persists in winter —
   uniform caps don't disambiguate.

Winter doesn't solve existing silhouette
ambiguity; it ADDS two new differentiators
(well/granary growth, unique tower+church caps)
and one new potential blur (well vs granary peak
convergence).
