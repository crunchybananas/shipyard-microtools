## the question

Given what 004 proved about `getDaylight()` — a rectangular 1.0
plateau across 42% of the day, a 0.55 night floor that was raised
from 0.3 for playability, and zero seasonal coupling — is this the
curve we'd pick if we were starting fresh?

Short answer: no, but the reasons matter.

## what the curve does well (honest first)

1. **High average brightness.** 100% of daylight at peak + 55%
   floor means the dimmest moment of the day is still bright
   enough to read. For a city-builder where the player must track
   dozens of tiny citizens, this is a gameplay feature, not a bug.
   Any re-shape that drops night below ~0.5 risks re-introducing
   the "can't see citizens at midnight" problem the 0.3→0.55 raise
   was fixing. That constraint is load-bearing.

2. **Predictable framing.** A rectangular plateau means screenshots
   taken between hours 4 and 14 look tonally identical. For a
   builder that wants to sell "look at my settlement," a long
   reliable golden-hour equivalent is actually useful. Stardew
   Valley does something similar — the sun-angle barely changes
   across its play-time window.

3. **The sky gradient is doing work even if `getDaylight` isn't.**
   `render.js:118-139` already mixes night/dawn/day/dusk colors
   into a top-to-bottom sky gradient with peaks at t=0.22 and
   t=0.78. So the sky *does* golden-hour even though the global
   luminance doesn't. The viewer's eye may register the warm sky
   wash as "the light shifting" without the ground tiles changing
   at all.

## what it does poorly

**Failure mode A — the frame lies about time.** From 003 Frame B
vs Frame A: winter "midday" (t=0.5, daylight=1.0, effLum=1.0)
and winter dusk (t=0.75, daylight=0.65, effLum=0.813) *should*
read as a 19% luminance difference. The photographer reported
them as "essentially the same tonal value." The numbers say they
aren't. That mismatch is the failure: the curve is "correct" but
the multiply-blue tint overlay (`render.js:2518` → roughly
`rgb(255-D×160, 255-D×150, 255-D×100)`) is dominant enough that
the luminance delta gets perceptually swallowed under the *same*
blue cast. **The dusk and midday frames look like the same
moment because they share the same dominant color, just at
different intensities.**

**Failure mode B — the plateau is shaped wrong for beauty.** A
10-hour flat 1.0 means "golden hour" has to be drawn by some
other mechanism (the sky gradient, per failure-mode-C). There
is never a *peak* — a moment the day reaches its best light and
starts declining. Instead we have a cliff at t=0.167 (daylight
snaps from 0.925 to 1.0 in one sample) and another cliff at
t=0.583 (1.0 → 0.844). Cliffs instead of arcs. **The player
never gets to say "oh, the light just changed."**

**Failure mode C — sky and ground are uncoupled.** Sky gradient
peaks warm at t=0.22 (dawn) and t=0.78 (dusk). Ground
luminance peaks flat at t=0.167–0.583. Those windows don't
overlap at all. At t=0.22 (warm dawn sky), ground is still in
the ramp (daylight ~0.925). At t=0.78 (warm dusk sky), ground is
already at 0.65. **You never see warm ground *with* warm sky.**
The two systems weren't designed together.

**Failure mode D — no seasonal coupling.** Winter sun is
identical to summer sun. Real winter has a lower, dimmer,
orange-warmer sun; real summer has a high, intense, white sun.
Realm's "winter" is currently a cool tile tint applied to the
same summer lighting. That's why 003 read winter as "under-
exposed" — the eye expects seasonally-correlated luminance.

## if I were champion-ing one change

**Do failure-mode C first.** Align the ground peak with the sky
warm moments. Concretely: the ground luminance should *rise*
through dawn (t=0.0–0.22) and *peak warm* briefly around t=0.22,
then *descend to a broad midday plateau* at t=0.3–0.55, then
*peak warm again* at t=0.78 before the night curve. Something
roughly like:

```
  t    current   proposed
  0.00   0.55      0.35   (darker dead of night — but briefly)
  0.10   1.00      0.70   (pre-dawn ramp start)
  0.22   1.00      1.05   (DAWN GOLDEN — warm-peak)
  0.40   1.00      0.95   (midday plateau, slightly cooler)
  0.60   1.00      0.95   (midday plateau ends)
  0.78   0.68      1.05   (DUSK GOLDEN — warm-peak)
  0.90   0.58      0.45   (night descent)
  1.00   0.55      0.35   (dead night)
```

The max goes to 1.05, not 1.0 — existing code multiplies this
value against alphas, so some call sites may need clamping. The
night floor drops to 0.35, but only briefly (t=0.92–0.08) — a
narrow "real dark" window before the sky gradient starts showing
dawn colors.

This is speculation, not a prescription. The shape could be
simple (two Gaussian peaks + a plateau) or a lookup table. What
matters is: ground peaks at dawn/dusk, not flat through midday.

## what NOT to do

1. **Don't raise the plateau past 1.0 globally.** Every call site
   in render.js assumes daylight ∈ [0, 1] and uses it as an alpha
   multiplier. Values > 1 will clip at the canvas level and
   silently produce no additional brightness. If you want warm
   peaks, do it via a separate `getWarmth(t)` function, not by
   overloading `getDaylight`.

2. **Don't drop the floor under 0.5 for normal play.** The
   0.3→0.55 raise was a *playability* fix, not a visual one. A
   photo-mode could toggle a deeper floor; gameplay should not.

3. **Don't season-couple without UI help.** If winter's peak drops
   to 0.9, winter midday will *feel* dimmer than summer — but the
   player won't know *why*. The current HUD shows the season
   emoji and name, but doesn't communicate "it's darker because
   it's winter." A season-coupled lighting change should ship
   with a brief "short winter days" tooltip or chronicle line.

## ideas generated → ideas.md

- Uncouple `getDaylight` from `getWarmth`; keep the first as a
  clamp [0,1] gameplay-safe value, make the second a free-range
  color bias.
- Align ground luminance peaks with sky-gradient peaks at t=0.22
  and t=0.78.
- Consider a `getSkyWarmth(t)` sampler on `G.debug` so the next
  profiler tick can confirm sky/ground alignment before we ship a
  change.
